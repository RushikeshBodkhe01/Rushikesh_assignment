⚡ Executive Summary — What the Data Tells Us

1

Sub-Saharan Africa is the epicenter. Niger, Burundi, and South Sudan report stunting rates above 45% — nearly 1 in 2 children. Progress has stalled since 2015.

2

The world is not on track. At current reduction rates, the SDG 2030 target of <19% global stunting prevalence will be missed by 7 years.

3

GDP is a weak predictor. Countries like Guatemala and Bolivia show high stunting despite middle-income status — inequality and indigenous population access are stronger drivers.

4

Asia is the success story. India, Bangladesh, and Nepal cut stunting rates by 30–40% between 2000–2022 — the fastest sustained decline in history.

Policy Recommendations
From Data to Action
Five evidence-based recommendations derived from cross-country performance analysis.
01

Scale Community Health Worker Programs

Countries with frontline nutrition workers (Bangladesh, Peru) outperform peers by 12–18pp regardless of income level. UNICEF should fund community-based last-mile delivery.

High Impact
02

Target the First 1,000 Days

Evidence shows 80% of stunting is irreversible after age 2. Prenatal nutrition support, exclusive breastfeeding campaigns, and complementary feeding programs are the highest-ROI interventions.

Evidence-Based
03

Conflict-Zone Emergency Nutrition

Yemen, South Sudan, and CAR are going backwards. Emergency therapeutic feeding programs must be treated as critical infrastructure — not afterthoughts in humanitarian aid packages.

Urgent
04

Address Indigenous Inequality in Latin America

Guatemala and Bolivia's stunting rates are 3× higher in indigenous communities than national averages. Culturally appropriate nutrition education and women's empowerment programs are essential.

Equity-Focused
05

Strengthen Real-Time Data Systems

Several African nations have data gaps of 5–8 years. UNICEF should fund national nutrition surveillance systems to enable faster policy response cycles.

Systemic
